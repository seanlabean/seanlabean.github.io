03R

Memorial day was the everything day. It started off badly when I consciously did not take out the trash the night before, thinking that the garbagemen would not come on a holiday (they never did in Philly). Turns out they do in Bloomington. 5am I was awoken to the sound of garbagemen shooting the shit and crashing around the truck. Too late for us, we'd have to live with an overflowing trashcan of rotting sun-baked garbage for another week. Then, I slept in, another bad sign. I hate losing the morning. Once we finally got rolling I received news from my mom that my grandfather was ailing rapidly. I was already planning to fly out to CA for my cousin's wedding the next weekend but didn't build in time to see gramps. So I quickly reorganized my travel to take me into SFO 3 days early and to stay a whole extra week. This would mean working from 4am to 2pm every day, but it would mean being able to see gramps and have time to spare to avoid a rushed travel.

Seeing gramps was tough. I have thought about the experience a lot but wont describe details here. I am so proud of my mom and my uncle for how much work she is doing to take care of him.

The wedding was nice. It was in Tahoe beneath the pines. It was fun to see my family all dressed up and having a good time. And luckily there were no breakdowns or stressful moments. I think it helps in a wedding when you know there are others who are infinitely more stressed than you.

03P

All our prep and thowing things away and packing things has led to this! Renna and I made the 12 hour drive from Philly to Bloominton, all in one go. It was around hour 8 that I started feeling the toll and the final hour and a half, most of which was in standstill Indianapolis traffic, was excruciating. All I wanted at that point was a nice well maintained hotel room. Needless to say, my reaction was far from positive when I rolled up to our 120 year old home, no furniture, doorknob falling off, and scuffed up floors. Beyond that, the act of then blowing up my air mattress to then sleep of the ravaged floors sealed the deal, I hated this place. BUT I want to make clear that my opinion of our home has changed drastically even just a week since then. Sure it's old and has its creaky quirks. But I actually enjoy how simple its construction is, especially how all the piping and wiring can be plainly seen if you go into the basement and look up. I was able to easily trace the coax cable back to the source and figure out why the internet was not working. Every pipe and gas line is apparent and easy to understand. I love how much easier it is to understand the house as a system, rather than having pipes and wires all around you like in an apartment complex. On top of that, the home has a brand new heater/AC, brand new gas stove, new fridge and washer. So it's got "good bones" as they say.

Bloomington is a pretty nice place too. I spent my first few work days nestled in the coworking space The Mill. At $100/mo I think it will give me a good place to feel accountable and surrounded by other hard working people, rather than be holed up at home all week. The food in Bloomington is questionable. The options are severely limited compared to Philly, there is one chinese place worth going, one mexican place, one thai place. This will take some getting used to, I hope to do much more cooking and baking at home. In the one week I've been here, I've met more people than I have in the last year in Philly. Everyone is very easy to talk to and more importantly very willing to share a 15 minute conversation. Life just seems to move a little slower here. It's back to school season, parents are leading their college kids around the (only) Target, and yet it didn't feel overcrowded or like I was in a mosh pit like I do most times back in Philly. This perception was put into stark contrast the other day when I was talking to a store attendant who had just graduated from Nashville Indiana high school, a 50 person class. They were commenting about how amazing Bloomington was but how crowded all the stores were this time of year, how incredible all the food choices were, but how crowded all the shops get this time of year. I couldn't believe it. But I guess it makes sense if you grew up and lived your whole life in a 3-stop-light town in southern Indiana.

Some good and some real bad happened thoughout this first week. The good: I was able to get a solid road bike for $100 and met an awesome bike shop owner who is fixing it up all nice, I met some new people who I hope to be friends with, our belongings were delivered mostly without a hitch and it makes the house feel much homier even with it all still in boxes. The bad: I went on a short walk through an unmaintained trail and my ankle brushed up against a tick nest. My ankle and foot were covered in hundreds of tiny poppyseed-sized ticks. I did my best to brush them off, rushed to the nearest CVS and lint roller-ed my whole body then washed with tick shampoo meant for dogs. Now I wait and see if any have got me and whether I've contracted Lyme or worse.

I had another burst of inspiration in and around my Vindauga project. In the last episode, I figured out how to render text to the screen and paired it as a caption to a series of photo album images. 03J

Vindauga has come a long way and I am pleased to report that it has reached a final draft of sorts. I imagine I will be adding many features to it and refactoring some of the clutter for many months to come but the core functionality is there. A few of the final core components were: scene memory - the ability to navigate backards through previously visited scenes, music, text rendering, clickable visualization with debug switch. I was also able to find a memory leak which was causing the footprint to grow linearly with time. One of the first enhancements I intend to make is to dynamically load the scene images only when they are navigated to, at the moment all images are loaded once at the start of the program.

Otherwise we are scambling to prepare for our major adventure of the year: a 2-week trip to Taiwan and China! Very excited for this.

03I

I've been working hard on my point-and-click SDL2 game. So far so good! It's been incredibly rewarding to meet weekly with the boys, it has been keeping my progress steady. And it's also cool to see what other people are working on and how they flex their skills to make something interesting. I comfortably created a little scene where clicking on a region displays a new image, right clicking returns you to the starting image, all while some atmospheric music plays in the background. I hit a couple snags trying to flesh the system out into something fully extensible, I am currently trying to figure out a way to track what image the player is currently looking at and what they were previously looking at so that right-clicking takes you back to that image, not just to the starting image. The boys had a good suggestion of using a tree structure of some sort. I am calling the game vindauga, old-norse for "wind's eye", which was subsumed into the English language as "window". I've been keeping a log of all my developments, using my forth-based HTML parser myrr. You can read the blog here.

Down and down.

I developed a passing interest in the Forth programming language. Fascinating construction and a very different way of thinking about computation. I followed some guides and wrote a text-parsing program which takes a custom Markdown-like file (I call it Myrrtext) and makes the text HTML compliant. The program is built to recognize special characters within the Myrrtext and jumps to and executes special Forth functions (a.k.a. words) which modify the text or insert HTML element markers. In this sense, the Myrrtext itself is self-modifying.
